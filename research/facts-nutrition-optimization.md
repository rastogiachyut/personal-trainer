## Protein Pacing & MPS Optimization

### Fact 1
- **Finding:** Older adults (>60y) require at least 2.8g leucine (~30g high-quality protein) per meal to stimulate muscle protein synthesis (MPS), while younger adults (<30y) show a near-linear MPS response proportional to protein content per meal, with no strict leucine threshold.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (multiple clinical studies, mechanism elucidated via mTORC1 pathway)
- **Source:** Layman DK, "Impacts of protein quantity and distribution on body composition," Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024 (cited by 31)
- **Practical Application:** At 28 years old, Achyut doesn't need to obsess over exact leucine thresholds — total per-meal protein (30-40g) is sufficient to maximize MPS. Just ensure each meal has enough complete protein.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 2
- **Finding:** The post-meal anabolic response of MPS has a finite duration of 2-3 hours. This means a single large protein dinner is suboptimal. Distribution across 4-5 meals spaced 3-4 hours apart maximizes total daily MPS.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (established mechanism, multiple studies)
- **Source:** Layman DK, Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024; Wilkinson et al., Physiological Reports, 2023 (systematic review, cited by 55)
- **Practical Application:** Aim for 4-5 protein-containing meals (breakfast, lunch, snack, dinner, optional pre-bed) each with 35-45g protein. For a 130 kg person targeting ~1.6g/kg lean mass, that's roughly 180-200g/day split across meals.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 3
- **Finding:** Total daily protein intake is the most important dietary factor affecting lean body mass. If total quantity is sufficiently high (≥1.6 g/kg/day), the relative importance of meal distribution and quality diminishes.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (consensus across multiple reviews and meta-analyses)
- **Source:** Layman DK, Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024; Morton et al. meta-analysis (1.62 g/kg/day threshold)
- **Practical Application:** Don't overthink distribution if total protein is on point. Hit ≥160g/day consistently first, then optimize distribution.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 4
- **Finding:** The leucine threshold hypothesis is supported by systematic review evidence: there is a dose-dependent association between dietary leucine intake and post-prandial post-exercise MPS rates. However, the relationship plateaus at higher doses.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (systematic review of acute MPS studies, cited by 55)
- **Source:** Wilkinson et al., "Association of postprandial postexercise muscle protein synthesis rates with dietary leucine: A systematic review," Physiological Reports, 2023
- **Practical Application:** Ensure 2-3g leucine per major meal (equivalent to ~30-40g whey, chicken, or eggs). Soya chunks also provide good leucine for vegetarian meals.
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 5
- **Finding:** Plant-based diets can supply enough protein and leucine to maximize hypertrophy and MPS, provided the leucine threshold of ~2g per meal is met through careful food combining and adequate total intake.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (narrative review with mechanistic analysis)
- **Source:** Goldman et al., "Completely plant-based diets that meet energy requirements for resistance training can supply enough protein and leucine to maximize hypertrophy," Nutrients, 2024 (cited by 10)
- **Practical Application:** Important for Indian vegetarian days — combine soya chunks, paneer, Greek yogurt, and legumes to hit leucine targets without needing meat every meal.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 6
- **Finding:** Consuming a balanced protein distribution (4-5 evenly spaced feedings of 30-40g each) is optimal for maximizing post-prandial MPS rates, compared to a skewed distribution (e.g., low breakfast, high dinner).
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (acute MPS studies, limited long-term RCTs)
- **Source:** Lak et al., Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024 (cited by 17); Areta et al. (classic 2013 study still referenced)
- **Practical Application:** Structure: Breakfast 40g, Lunch 40g, Snack 30g, Dinner 50g, Pre-bed 30g = ~190g total
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 7
- **Finding:** Protein quality (DIAAS/PDCAAS) matters more when total protein intake is marginal. At higher intakes (≥1.6g/kg/day), the quality of individual protein sources becomes less critical because total amino acid supply is adequate.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate
- **Source:** Layman DK, Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024; Monteyne et al., Journal of Nutrition, 2023 (cited by 95 — vegan vs omnivorous high-protein diets produced comparable daily myofibrillar PS rates)
- **Practical Application:** Don't stress about combining plant proteins at every meal if total intake is 180g+. Just vary sources across the day.
- **Relevance:** Medium

## Nutrient Timing (Pre/Intra/Post-Workout)

### Fact 8
- **Finding:** The 30-minute "anabolic window" is largely a myth. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis found that protein timing around exercise does not influence muscular adaptations when total daily protein intake is matched.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (systematic review with meta-analysis, cited by 6)
- **Source:** Casuso & Goossens, "Does protein ingestion timing affect exercise-induced adaptations? A systematic review with meta-analysis," Nutrients, 2025
- **Practical Application:** Don't stress about chugging a protein shake within 30 minutes of training. Just ensure total daily protein is adequate. Getting protein within 1-2 hours pre or post is fine.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 9
- **Finding:** An 8-week RCT comparing protein intake immediately before/after vs. 3 hours before/after resistance training found NO significant between-group differences in muscle mass, strength, or performance gains. Both groups improved equally.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (randomized clinical trial, protein-matched control)
- **Source:** Lak et al., "Timing matters?...," Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024 (cited by 17)
- **Practical Application:** Train when convenient. Eat protein when convenient. Just hit daily targets consistently.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 10
- **Finding:** Previous meta-analyses on protein timing (which suggested timing matters) were limited by inactive comparator designs — the "control" groups often received no protein at all, making it impossible to separate timing effects from total protein effects.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** Lak et al., Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024 (discusses methodological limitations of prior reviews); Schoenfeld et al. (2013 meta-analysis, now recognized as having this limitation)
- **Practical Application:** The supplement industry's push for precise timing is based on flawed studies. Focus on what matters: total protein, total calories, progressive overload.
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 11
- **Finding:** While the anabolic window is wider than once thought (~4-6 hours post-exercise), there may still be a modest benefit to consuming protein within a few hours after training, particularly in a fasted state or when the pre-workout meal was >4 hours prior.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (mechanistic MPS data, no strong long-term hypertrophy evidence)
- **Source:** Deane et al., "Critical variables regulating age-related anabolic responses to protein nutrition in skeletal muscle," Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024 (cited by 30)
- **Practical Application:** If training first thing in the morning (fasted), eat protein within 1-2 hours post-workout. If training 1-2 hours after a meal, the post-workout shake is optional.
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 12
- **Finding:** The daily protein threshold for maximizing hypertrophy is ~1.62 g/kg bodyweight/day (from the Morton et al. meta-analysis). Beyond this, additional protein provides diminishing returns for muscle growth specifically.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (meta-analysis of 49 studies)
- **Source:** Morton et al., British Journal of Sports Medicine, 2018 (still the definitive meta-analysis, referenced throughout 2022-2025 literature)
- **Practical Application:** For a 130 kg person, target 1.6g/kg of lean/ideal body mass, not total body weight. If using total weight, that's 208g — close to our 180-200g target using lean mass estimates.
- **Relevance:** High

## Carb Cycling for Recomp

### Fact 13
- **Finding:** Carb cycling (alternating high-carb training days with low-carb rest days) lacks direct systematic review or meta-analysis evidence demonstrating superiority over consistent caloric intake for body recomposition. The practice is primarily supported by bodybuilding tradition and mechanistic rationale rather than rigorous RCTs.
- **Evidence:** 🔴 Weak (no direct meta-analyses; mostly narrative reviews and expert opinion)
- **Source:** Valentyna, "Carbohydrate Cycling as a Tool for Managing Body Composition in the Pre-Contest Phase," Universal Library of Innovative Research, 2025; Wachsmuth et al., Nutrients, 2022 (cited by 54, mentions carb cycling but doesn't test it directly)
- **Practical Application:** For a beginner, carb cycling adds unnecessary complexity. A consistent moderate deficit with adequate protein is simpler and equally effective. Reserve carb cycling for advanced leaning-out phases.
- **Relevance:** Low (at current training stage)

### Fact 14
- **Finding:** A crossover RCT comparing high-carb/low-fat vs. low-carb diets found that performance in endurance and high-intensity exercise was better maintained on high-carb, but body composition changes (fat mass) were similar between diets when protein and calories were matched.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (RCT, crossover design, cited by 54)
- **Source:** Wachsmuth et al., "The impact of a high-carbohydrate/low fat vs. low-carbohydrate diet on performance and body composition in physically active adults," Nutrients, 2022
- **Practical Application:** Keep carbs moderate-to-high on training days for performance. Don't go full keto — it may hurt training intensity without improving fat loss when calories are matched.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 15
- **Finding:** An RCT of a mobile app-based program combining intermittent fasting + carb cycling + exercise (FASTer Way) showed statistically significant improvements in body composition, fat mass, and lean mass. However, it's unclear which component (IF, carb cycling, or exercise) drove the results.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (RCT but multi-component intervention, cited by 10+)
- **Source:** Ottinger et al., "A randomized controlled trial to assess the effects of a mobile application-based lifestyle change program (FASTer Way)," mHealth, 2025
- **Practical Application:** The overall approach (caloric control + exercise + protein) works. Whether carb cycling specifically adds value is unclear.
- **Relevance:** Low

### Fact 16
- **Finding:** During caloric restriction, the key factors for sparing lean mass are: sufficient protein intake (≥1.6g/kg), adequate resistance training volume, and a moderate (not aggressive) caloric deficit. Carbohydrate manipulation (cycling) appears to be a secondary factor.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (systematic analysis)
- **Source:** Roth et al., "Lean mass sparing in resistance-trained athletes during caloric restriction: the role of resistance training volume," European Journal of Applied Physiology, 2022 (cited by 33)
- **Practical Application:** For recomp: protein ≥180g/day, resistance training 4x/week, moderate deficit (~500 kcal). These fundamentals matter far more than carb timing.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 17
- **Finding:** Caloric restriction combined with resistance training can achieve simultaneous fat loss and lean mass gain (true recomposition), especially in beginners, overweight individuals, and those returning to training. Carb cycling is not required for this effect.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (well-established in literature)
- **Source:** Baranauskas et al., Nutrients, 2023 (cited by 30); Fernandes, Science & Sports, 2023 (cited by 4)
- **Practical Application:** As a 130 kg beginner, recomposition is very achievable. Focus on consistent deficit + high protein + progressive overload. Save carb cycling for when progress stalls months in.
- **Relevance:** High

## Sodium & Electrolyte Timing

### Fact 18
- **Finding:** Sodium losses through sweat during exercise can reach 500-2000mg per hour depending on intensity, heat, and individual sweat rate. At a sweat rate of 1L/hour, sodium loss is approximately 2% of total body sodium per hour.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** Minson & Halliwill, "Fluid and Electrolyte Replacement," in Nutrition, Water, and Electrolytes in Sports, 2023 (textbook chapter)
- **Practical Application:** For intense training sessions lasting >60 minutes, consume 300-500mg sodium (in water) pre-workout and consider intra-workout electrolytes for sessions >90 minutes.
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 19
- **Finding:** Pre-workout sodium loading (500-1000mg sodium in 500-750ml water, 60-90 minutes pre-exercise) enhances plasma volume, improves pump during resistance training, and helps maintain performance during long or hot sessions.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (physiological studies, sports nutrition consensus)
- **Source:** Blow et al., "Hydration and Electrolytes for an Athlete," in Integrative Sport and Exercise Physiology, 2025; ISSN exercise position stands
- **Practical Application:** Mix 1/4 tsp salt (≈500mg sodium) in 500ml water before training. This is especially useful on hot days in Ghaziabad (summer temps 40°C+).
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 20
- **Finding:** Exercise-associated muscle cramping (EAMC) is primarily caused by muscle fatigue and neuromuscular control issues, NOT primarily by dehydration/electrolyte imbalance as commonly believed. However, sodium depletion can contribute in prolonged exercise in heat.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (multiple studies, shift in consensus since 2010s)
- **Source:** Minson & Halliwill, 2023 textbook; Schwellnus (original EAMC etiology research)
- **Practical Application:** If cramping occurs during lifting, focus on proper warm-up, technique, and rest between sets. Don't blame salt alone. But do maintain baseline hydration.
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 21
- **Finding:** The co-transport of water and sodium in the small intestine promotes renal reabsorption of water, enhancing hydration more effectively than plain water alone during exercise.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** Liang et al., "Consumption of a BCAA-containing sports beverage during 21km running reduces dehydration," Nutrients, 2024
- **Practical Application:** For long sessions, add a pinch of salt + some glucose to water rather than drinking plain water. This is the basis of ORS — and the same principle applies to training hydration.
- **Relevance:** Low (for typical 45-60 min lifting sessions)

### Fact 22
- **Finding:** The ISSN and major sports nutrition bodies recommend 300-600mg sodium per hour of exercise for activities lasting >1 hour, taken with fluids. For resistance training sessions under 60 minutes, normal dietary sodium intake is usually sufficient.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (consensus statements)
- **Source:** Sports nutrition consensus statements (ISSN, ACSM, NATA)
- **Practical Application:** For 45-60 min gym sessions, pre-workout salt is optional. Just ensure adequate sodium intake across meals (Indian food is typically well-salted).
- **Relevance:** Medium

## Fasted vs Fed Training

### Fact 23
- **Finding:** A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis found that resistance training performed in the fasted state vs. fed state produces similar gains in fat-free mass, muscle hypertrophy, and strength, provided total daily protein and calories are matched.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (systematic review with meta-analysis)
- **Source:** Vieira et al., "Resistance training performed in the fasted state compared to the fed state on body composition and strength in adults: A systematic review with meta-analysis," Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies, 2025
- **Practical Application:** Training fasted (e.g., early morning before eating) is fine for muscle growth as long as total daily nutrition is on point. Just eat a high-protein meal afterward.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 24
- **Finding:** A randomized clinical trial found that fasted vs. fed resistance training resulted in similar muscle hypertrophy and strength gains. Fasted training did NOT impair muscle growth.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (RCT, cited by 6)
- **Source:** Vieira et al., "Impact of overnight fasted state versus fed state on adaptations to resistance training: a randomized clinical trial," IJSNEM, 2025
- **Practical Application:** If mornings work best for consistency and you prefer training fasted, go ahead. Consistency > timing of meals around training.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 25
- **Finding:** A study on Ramadan fasting (training in fasted vs. fed conditions) found that both groups maintained muscle strength and hypertrophy. There were slightly larger increases in muscle circumferences when exercise was performed in the fed state, but differences were not significant.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (cited by 28)
- **Source:** Triki et al., "Timing of resistance training during Ramadan fasting and its effects on muscle strength and hypertrophy," IJSPP, 2023
- **Practical Application:** Training during Ramadan or other fasted conditions is safe and effective for hypertrophy.
- **Relevance:** Low (informational)

### Fact 26
- **Finding:** While fasted training doesn't impair hypertrophy in response to total daily nutrition, there may be a modest advantage to being in a fed state for acute MPS response — exercise enhances the anabolic sensitivity of muscle to subsequent protein feeding for up to 24 hours.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (acute MPS studies)
- **Source:** Camera, "Evaluating the effects of increased protein intake on muscle strength, hypertrophy and power adaptations," Sports Medicine, 2022 (cited by 34)
- **Practical Application:** If training fasted, prioritize a high-protein meal within 1-2 hours post-workout to take advantage of the enhanced MPS sensitivity window.
- **Relevance:** Medium

## Alcohol's Dose-Dependent Effects on MPS

### Fact 27
- **Finding:** Both acute and chronic alcohol use decrease muscle protein synthesis and increase protein degradation. Alcohol impairs the mTORC1 signaling pathway, which is the primary driver of MPS in response to resistance training and amino acids.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (comprehensive review of 96 papers)
- **Source:** Simon et al., "Alcohol and Skeletal Muscle in Health and Disease," Alcohol Research: Current Reviews, 2023 (PMC10627576)
- **Practical Application:** Any alcohol consumption will blunt the anabolic response to training. Minimize or eliminate alcohol during serious training phases.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 28
- **Finding:** A single binge-drinking session (BAC ≥0.08 g/dL — equivalent to ~4-5 standard drinks for men) can cause acute alcohol-related myopathy, including rhabdomyolysis. Even at sub-rhabdo levels, a single binge impairs MPS for up to 24+ hours post-consumption.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** Simon et al., Alcohol Research: Current Reviews, 2023
- **Practical Application:** Binge drinking after a training session is the worst-case scenario for muscle growth. Even one heavy night out can erase that week's training adaptations.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 29
- **Finding:** 40-60% of people with chronic heavy alcohol misuse develop alcohol-related myopathy (muscle wasting). Chronic alcohol use decreases both protein synthesis (via mTORC1 inhibition) and increases protein breakdown (via ubiquitin-proteasome pathway activation, increased atrogin-1/MuRF-1).
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** Simon et al., Alcohol Research: Current Reviews, 2023; Meza et al., Alcohol and Alcoholism, 2022 (cited by 151)
- **Practical Application:** Chronic drinking (even moderate-heavy, 3-4+ drinks/day regularly) will undermine muscle gain and fat loss goals.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 30
- **Finding:** Alcohol impairs mitochondrial function in skeletal muscle, reducing ATP production capacity. This directly affects training performance and recovery between sets.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** Simon et al., Alcohol Research: Current Reviews, 2023; Dominguez et al., "Alcoholic cardiomyopathy: an update," European Heart Journal, 2024 (cited by 117)
- **Practical Application:** Even moderate alcohol the night before training can reduce workout quality. Avoid alcohol 24 hours before heavy sessions.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 31
- **Finding:** Post-exercise alcohol consumption (1.0 g ethanol/kg bodyweight — roughly 5-6 drinks for an 80kg male) significantly delays strength recovery after resistance exercise. Even lower doses (0.5 g/kg) may impair recovery in some individuals.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (individual studies, preclinical + limited human data)
- **Source:** Bourgeois et al., "Chronic alcohol and skeletal muscle," Handbook of Substance Misuse, 2022
- **Practical Application:** If drinking after a workout (social event), keep it to 1-2 drinks max. 5+ drinks will measurably impair recovery.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 32
- **Finding:** Abstinence or reducing alcohol use can improve muscle mass and function, but muscles are NOT fully restored to normal levels even after cessation — some chronic damage appears irreversible.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate
- **Source:** Simon et al., Alcohol Research: Current Reviews, 2023
- **Practical Application:** The sooner you cut back, the better. Long-term heavy drinking creates lasting muscle damage that exercise alone can't fully fix.
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 33
- **Finding:** Alcohol's effect on MPS is dose-dependent: low-moderate intake (1-2 drinks) appears to have minimal impact on MPS when adequate protein is consumed, while higher doses (≥0.5g ethanol/kg) cause significant MPS suppression (24-37% reduction in some studies).
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (limited human dose-response studies)
- **Source:** Review by Bourgeois et al., Handbook of Substance Misuse, 2022; Simon et al., 2023
- **Practical Application:** 1-2 occasional drinks with a high-protein meal is unlikely to derail progress. The problem is binge drinking (4+ drinks), especially post-workout.
- **Relevance:** High

## Indian High-Protein Diet Engineering (Cross-Reference)

### Fact 34
- **Finding:** Soya chunks (nutrela) provide the highest protein-per-rupee of any commonly available protein source in India at ~52g protein per 100g (dry) and cost of approximately ₹0.15-0.20 per gram of protein.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (nutritional databases, market pricing)
- **Source:** Indian Food Composition Database (IFCT 2017); market pricing (Blinkit/Zepto, Ghaziabad, 2026)
- **Practical Application:** Use 50-100g soya chunks (dry weight) as a daily protein base. Soak, squeeze, and cook with Indian spices. 100g dry = 52g protein for ~₹10-15.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 35
- **Finding:** Whey protein concentrate is the most cost-effective supplement in India at ₹1.5-2.5 per gram of protein (for quality brands like As It Is, MyProtein, Nakpro). One 30g scoop provides ~24g protein.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** Market analysis (Amazon India, Flipkart pricing, 2025-2026)
- **Practical Application:** 2 scoops/day (48g protein) costs ~₹100-120/day. This is the backbone of an affordable high-protein Indian diet.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 36
- **Finding:** Paneer provides ~18g protein and ~20g fat per 100g. While tasty and versatile, it is NOT a cost-effective protein source due to high fat content (calorie-dense). Low-fat paneer or Greek yogurt (hung curd) is a better option for recomp.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (nutritional databases)
- **Source:** IFCT 2017; USDA FoodData Central
- **Practical Application:** Use paneer as a flavor/satisfaction food, not a primary protein source during recomp. 100g paneer = 260+ kcal for only 18g protein. Greek yogurt (hung curd) is far better: 10g protein per 100g at only 60 kcal.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 37
- **Finding:** Eggs provide ~6g protein each at a cost of ~₹0.8-1.0 per gram of protein (at ₹7-8/egg). A 6-egg omelette provides 36g protein for ~₹42-48 and ~420 kcal (with minimal cooking oil).
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** Market pricing (Ghaziabad, 2026); IFCT 2017
- **Practical Application:** 4-6 whole eggs for breakfast = 24-36g protein. Combine with 2 slices whole wheat bread and vegetables for a complete high-protein Indian breakfast.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 38
- **Finding:** Chicken breast is the leanest animal protein source available in India at ~31g protein and only 3.6g fat per 100g (165 kcal). Cost: ~₹1.5-2.0 per gram of protein at ₹250-300/kg.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong
- **Source:** IFCT 2017; market pricing (Ghaziabad, 2026)
- **Practical Application:** 250g chicken breast = 77g protein for only 412 kcal. Ideal for dinner on training days.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 39
- **Finding:** Indian breakfast foods (poha, upma, idli, dosa) are traditionally very low in protein (5-8g per serving). Simple modifications can boost protein to 25-35g per serving: add whey protein, soya chunks, Greek yogurt, or extra eggs.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (recipe analysis, not peer-reviewed)
- **Source:** Recipe analysis based on IFCT nutritional data
- **Practical Application:** High-protein poha: 1 cup flattened rice + 50g soya chunks + peanuts + whey-mixed water = ~35g protein. High-protein besan cheela: 100g besan + 2 eggs + vegetables = ~30g protein.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 40
- **Finding:** A completely vegetarian Indian diet can achieve 180g+ protein per day at under 2,200 kcal by combining: whey (2 scoops = 48g), soya chunks (100g dry = 52g), Greek yogurt/hung curd (300g = 30g), paneer (100g = 18g), lentils/dal (200g cooked = 14g), milk (500ml = 18g), and assorted vegetables/grains (remaining ~20g).
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate (meal plan analysis)
- **Source:** Calculated from IFCT 2017 nutritional data
- **Practical Application:** This meal plan costs approximately ₹200-250/day and is fully vegetarian. Add 200g chicken breast to replace paneer for even better protein:calorie ratio.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 41
- **Finding:** Lentils and legumes (dal, chana, rajma, moong), while healthy, are relatively poor protein sources per calorie: 200g cooked dal = only ~14-18g protein for 230+ kcal. They should not be counted as primary protein sources during a recomp phase.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (nutritional databases)
- **Source:** IFCT 2017; USDA FoodData Central
- **Practical Application:** Eat dal/chana for fiber, micronutrients, and satiety — not as a main protein source. Count them toward total protein but don't rely on them.
- **Relevance:** Medium

## Cross-Topic Practical Facts

### Fact 42
- **Finding:** For body recomposition (simultaneous fat loss + muscle gain), the evidence-based hierarchy of importance is: 1) Progressive resistance training, 2) Total daily protein (≥1.6g/kg), 3) Caloric balance (slight deficit), 4) Sleep (7-9h), 5) Protein distribution, 6) Nutrient timing, 7) Carb cycling, 8) Supplements.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (consensus across multiple systematic reviews)
- **Source:** Synthesized from: Roth et al., EJAP, 2022; Morton et al., BJSM, 2018; Layman, Frontiers, 2024; Lak et al., Frontiers, 2024
- **Practical Application:** Don't optimize #6-8 until #1-4 are dialed in consistently for weeks.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 43
- **Finding:** The ISSN recommends consuming 20-40g of high-quality protein per meal, 4-5 times daily, spaced 3-4 hours apart, with a total daily intake of 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day for resistance-trained individuals. This aligns with all current evidence.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (position stand)
- **Source:** Jäger et al., International Society of Sports Nutrition Position Stand: Protein and Exercise, JISSN, 2017 (still current, reaffirmed in subsequent literature)
- **Practical Application:** Use this as the master template. 4 meals × 40g + 1 snack × 20g = 180g/day.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 44
- **Finding:** Post-exercise, consuming protein + carbohydrate together does not appear to enhance MPS or long-term hypertrophy beyond protein alone, when total daily carbohydrate and protein are matched.
- **Evidence:** 🟡 Moderate
- **Source:** Staples et al. (and subsequent confirmations); also supported by Casuso & Goossens, 2025 meta-analysis
- **Practical Application:** Don't worry about a specific carb:protein ratio post-workout. Just eat a normal meal containing both within a few hours.
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 45
- **Finding:** Creatine monohydrate (3-5g/day) is the most evidence-backed supplement for increasing lean mass and strength, with hundreds of RCTs confirming its efficacy. It works by increasing phosphocreatine availability and cell volume/hydration.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (hundreds of RCTs, multiple meta-analyses)
- **Source:** IKIU/ISSN position stands; Hespel et al. reviews
- **Practical Application:** 5g creatine monohydrate daily, any time of day. No loading phase needed. Costs ~₹300-500/month in India.
- **Relevance:** High

### Fact 46
- **Finding:** Caffeine (3-6mg/kg, 30-60 min pre-workout) reliably improves resistance training performance, power output, and perceived energy. For an 80kg person, that's 240-480mg caffeine (2-3 cups strong coffee or 1 pre-workout drink).
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (well-established ergogenic aid)
- **Source:** Grgic et al., multiple meta-analyses on caffeine and resistance exercise performance
- **Practical Application:** Strong black coffee 45 min before training. If training late evening, skip it to avoid sleep disruption.
- **Relevance:** Medium

### Fact 47
- **Finding:** Sleep deprivation (≤5.5h/night) reduces MPS and increases cortisol, accelerating muscle loss and impairing recovery. Even one night of poor sleep can reduce next-day training performance by 5-10%.
- **Evidence:** 🟢 Strong (multiple RCTs)
- **Source:** Dattilo et al., Medical Hypotheses, 2011 (foundational); multiple 2022-2025 confirmations in exercise science literature
- **Practical Application:** 7-9 hours of sleep is non-negotiable for recomp. It's as important as training and nutrition.
- **Relevance:** High

## Summary of Most Actionable Findings

1. **Total protein > timing > distribution** — Hit 180g+/day consistently first. 4-5 meals of 30-45g is optimal but not critical vs just hitting the total.
2. **The anabolic window is 4-6 hours, not 30 minutes** — Don't stress about post-workout shakes.
3. **Fasted training is fine** — If mornings work best, train fasted. Just eat protein-rich meals afterward.
4. **Alcohol is dose-dependent poison for gains** — 1-2 drinks = minimal impact. 4+ drinks = significant MPS impairment.
5. **Carb cycling is unnecessary for beginners** — Focus on consistent deficit + protein + training first.
6. **Pre-workout salt helps** — 500mg sodium in water before training improves pump and hydration (especially in Indian heat).
7. **Whey + soya chunks + eggs + chicken = cheapest high-protein Indian diet** — These four sources provide the best protein-per-rupee.
