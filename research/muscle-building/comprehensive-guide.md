## 1. Hypertrophy Fundamentals

### What Drives Muscle Growth?

Muscle hypertrophy is an increase in skeletal muscle cross-sectional area, achieved primarily through resistance training. The scientific literature identifies **three primary mechanisms** of hypertrophy:

#### The Three Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Role | How to Maximize |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanical Tension** | The **primary driver** of hypertrophy. Force generated by muscle fibers against resistance triggers mTOR signaling and protein synthesis pathways. | Progressive overload through full range of motion, loads in the 6-20 rep range (30-85% 1RM), controlled tempo |
| **Metabolic Stress** | Accumulation of metabolites (lactate, H+, phosphate) during sustained muscular work. Triggers anabolic signaling, growth hormone release, and cellular swelling. | Higher rep ranges (12-20+), shorter rest periods (30-90 sec), constant tension, "pump" techniques |
| **Muscle Damage** | Micro-tears in muscle fibers from eccentric (lowering) phases and unfamiliar movements. Triggers inflammatory response and satellite cell activation. | Emphasis on eccentric control, full range of motion, novel exercise variations |

> **Key Science:** A 2025 review in *Trends in Endocrinology and Metabolism* states: "Mechanical tension is widely recognized as the primary stimulus underlying the molecular mechanisms that influence muscle hypertrophy induced by resistance training" ([Schoenfeld, 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2095254625000869)). The same review notes that claims about acute hormonal responses being major drivers of hypertrophy are "outdated or overstated."

### Progressive Overload — The Master Principle

Progressive overload is the gradual increase of stress placed upon the body during exercise. Without it, muscle growth stalls. It can be achieved through:

1. **Increasing load** (more weight on the bar)
2. **Increasing reps** (more repetitions at the same weight)
3. **Increasing sets** (more total volume)
4. **Improving technique** (better range of motion = more mechanical tension)
5. **Increasing frequency** (training a muscle more often)
6. **Decreasing rest** (more metabolic stress)

For a beginner, **progressive overload happens rapidly** — neurological adaptations allow strength gains within the first 2-4 weeks, followed by muscle tissue adaptations from weeks 6-12 onward.

### Volume — How Much Is Enough?

**Training volume** = sets × reps × weight. The most useful metric is **hard sets per muscle group per week** (sets taken close to failure).

- **Beginners:** 8-12 hard sets per muscle group per week is sufficient and optimal
- **Intermediate:** 12-16 sets per muscle group per week
- **Advanced:** 16-20+ sets per muscle group per week

A 2022 umbrella review (Bernárdez-Vázquez et al., *Frontiers in Sports and Active Living*) analyzed 14 meta-analyses comprising 178 primary studies and 4,784 participants, concluding: **"At least 10 sets per week per muscle group is optimal"** for hypertrophy ([source](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9302196/)).

The Schoenfeld et al. (2017) meta-analysis found a **dose-response relationship** between weekly volume and hypertrophy, with greater growth at higher set counts. However, returns diminish — and excessive volume leads to junk volume and impaired recovery.

**For a 130 kg beginner:** Start at the **lower end (8-10 sets per muscle group per week)**. Beginners don't need high volume — they need *consistent* volume with progressive overload.

### Frequency — How Often?

Research shows that training each muscle group **2-3 times per week** produces greater hypertrophy than once per week, when volume is equated.

- Schoenfeld et al. (2016) meta-analysis: Training a muscle ≥2×/week resulted in **significantly greater hypertrophy** than 1×/week at equal volumes ([source](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-016-0543-8)).
- A subsequent review (Grgic et al., 2018) found that **when volume is equated, frequency's effect is minimal** — suggesting frequency matters mostly because it allows better volume distribution.

**Practical takeaway:** For a beginner, 2-3 sessions per week hitting full-body or upper/lower splits is ideal.

### Intensity — How Hard?

Intensity in hypertrophy has two meanings:

1. **Load intensity** (% of 1RM): Hypertrophy can occur across a wide range — from 30% to 85% of 1RM. The key is **proximity to failure**.
2. **Effort intensity (RIR — Reps in Reserve):** This is what matters most. Training within **1-3 Reps in Reserve (RIR)** of muscular failure maximizes hypertrophy.

**RIR Scale:**
- RIR 0 = Absolute failure (cannot complete another rep)
- RIR 1 = Could do 1 more rep
- RIR 2 = Could do 2 more reps
- RIR 3 = Could do 3 more reps

**For beginners:** Start at **RIR 2-3** for the first 4-6 weeks (learning form, building tissue tolerance), then progress to **RIR 1-2** as technique mastery improves.

### Rep Range — What's Optimal?

The evidence is clear: **there is no single "hypertrophy rep range."** As long as sets are taken close to failure, hypertrophy occurs across a wide spectrum:

- **5-8 reps:** Heavy, high mechanical tension. Good for compound lifts.
- **8-15 reps:** The "sweet spot" — high mechanical tension + meaningful metabolic stress.
- **15-25+ reps:** Light weights, high metabolic stress. Useful for isolation exercises and joint-friendly options.

A 2022 umbrella review concluded: **"There is no ideal load prescription to maximize muscle hypertrophy"** when effort is equated ([Schoenfeld et al., 2017](https://journals.lww.com/nsca-scj/fulltext/2017/10000/muscle_hypertrophy__a_narrative_review_on_training.8.aspx)).

**For a 130 kg beginner:** Favor the **10-15 rep range** initially. This allows:
- Lighter absolute loads (joint protection)
- More practice repetitions (motor learning)
- Lower injury risk
- Meaningful metabolic stress

## 2. Training for Beginners at High Body Weight

### Joint Considerations at 130 kg

Carrying 130 kg of body weight creates unique challenges:

| Area | Concern | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Knees** | Squatting/lunging with 130 kg bodyweight puts enormous patellofemoral force on knees | Use machines (leg press, knee extension) initially; limit deep squats; favor partial ranges |
| **Lower Back** | Bodyweight + weak core = high lumbar shear during bent-over movements | Avoid barbell rows/deadlifts initially; use seated cable rows, chest-supported rows; prioritize core stability |
| **Hips/Mobility** | Excess tissue limits hip ROM, making deep squats and conventional deadlifts unsafe | Use box squats, partial ranges, sumo stance; improve hip mobility gradually |
| **Shoulders** | Limited ROM may make overhead pressing uncomfortable | Use partial ranges, machine shoulder press, neutral grips |
| **Wrists/Elbows** | Excess body weight during push-ups/planks can stress small joints | Start with machine pressing; use dumbbells with neutral grips |
| **Cardiovascular** | Higher heart rate response to exercise; lower work capacity | Keep rest periods adequate (90-120 sec); monitor perceived exertion; avoid HIIT initially |

### Machine vs. Free Weight vs. Bodyweight

#### Machines — **START HERE**
- **Pros:** Fixed movement path (safety), no balance requirement, easy to learn, isolates muscles, lower injury risk, scalable resistance
- **Cons:** Less functional carryover, limited core activation
- **Best for a 130 kg beginner:** Nearly everything for the first 4-8 weeks

#### Free Weights (Dumbbells) — **Phase 2 (Week 6+)**
- **Pros:** Greater range of motion, stabilizer activation, unilateral correction
- **Cons:** Requires technique, balance, and core stability
- **Best for a 130 kg beginner:** Dumbbell presses (flat/incline), dumbbell rows (chest-supported), goblet squats (partial range)

#### Free Weights (Barbell) — **Phase 3 (Month 3+ or later)**
- **Pros:** Greatest load potential, efficiency for compound movements
- **Cons:** Highest technical demand, highest injury risk for beginners at high body weight
- **Recommendation:** **Delay barbells** until body weight is reduced and movement competency is established

#### Bodyweight Exercises — **Use Selectively**
At 130 kg, many bodyweight exercises are **extremely challenging or contraindicated**:
- ❌ Push-ups: Excessive load on wrists/shoulders at this weight
- ❌ Pull-ups: Likely impossible at 130 kg
- ❌ Lunges: High knee stress
- ❌ Burpees: High cardiovascular and joint stress
- ✅ Wall push-ups: Scalable
- ✅ Box squats (to a bench): Controlled depth
- ✅ Banded rows/pulldowns: Scalable resistance
- ✅ Glute bridges: Joint-friendly, scalable

### Safe Exercises for 130 kg Beginners

#### Lower Body
| Exercise | Why It's Safe | How to Progress |
|---|---|---|
| Leg Press (machine) | Back supported, controlled path, scalable | Start light, 10-15 reps, increase load weekly |
| Leg Extension (machine) | Zero spinal load, isolates quads | High rep (15-20), low load initially |
| Lying Leg Curl (machine) | No spinal loading, isolates hamstrings | 12-15 reps |
| Glute Bridge (floor) | Joint-friendly, strengthens posterior chain | Add resistance band, then barbell later |
| Calf Raise (machine/seated) | Low joint stress | 15-20 reps |

#### Upper Body Push
| Exercise | Why It's Safe | How to Progress |
|---|---|---|
| Machine Chest Press | Fixed path, no balance needed | 10-15 reps, increase load weekly |
| Dumbbell Flat Press (later) | Greater ROM, stabilizer work | Start with light dumbbells, 10-12 reps |
| Incline Machine Press | Targets upper chest safely | 10-15 reps |
| Machine Shoulder Press | Back supported, controlled | 10-12 reps |
| Triceps Pushdown (cable) | Isolates triceps, no joint stress | 12-15 reps |

#### Upper Body Pull
| Exercise | Why It's Safe | How to Progress |
|---|---|---|
| Lat Pulldown (cable) | Scalable, builds pulling strength | 10-15 reps, work toward bodyweight pull-ups long-term |
| Seated Cable Row | Chest support protects lower back | 10-15 reps |
| Face Pulls (cable) | Posterior delts, rotator cuff health | 15-20 reps, light load |
| Biceps Curl (cable/dumbbell) | Isolation, low risk | 12-15 reps |

#### Core
| Exercise | Why It's Safe | How to Progress |
|---|---|---|
| Plank (from knees) | Scalable, builds anti-extension | Hold 20-30 sec, progress to full plank |
| Dead Bug | Supine (safe for back), teaches bracing | 10 reps per side |
| Bird Dog | Quadruped, builds anti-rotation | 10 reps per side |
| Cable Pallof Press | Anti-rotation, scalable | 10-12 reps per side |

### How to Start Without Injury — The First 4 Weeks

**Non-negotiable rules:**

1. **Warm up 5-10 minutes** before every session (brisk walk, arm circles, leg swings)
2. **Start with 50-60% of what you think you can lift** — err on the side of too light
3. **Focus on technique over load** for the first 4 weeks
4. **Never train through sharp pain** (muscle burn is OK; joint pain is not)
5. **Use RIR 3 for week 1-2, RIR 2-3 for week 3-4** — don't go to failure yet
6. **Rest 90-120 seconds between sets** — longer than typical because cardiovascular fitness is lower
7. **End each session with 5 minutes of gentle stretching**

## 3. Body Recomposition

### Can You Build Muscle While Losing Fat?

**Yes — and for this specific profile (beginner, high body fat, 130 kg), body recomposition is not just possible, it's the most likely outcome.**

### What the Science Says

#### Evidence FOR Recomposition

1. **Barakat et al. (2020)** in the *NSCA Strength & Conditioning Journal* reviewed the literature and concluded: "Although many suggest that [body recomposition] only occurs in untrained/novice and overweight/obese populations, there is a substantial amount of literature demonstrating this body recomposition phenomenon in resistance-trained individuals" too ([source](https://journals.lww.com/nsca-scj/Fulltext/2020/10000/Body_Recomposition__Can_Trained_Individuals_Build.3.aspx)).

2. **A 2026 study in the *European Journal of Applied Physiology*** compared two nutritional protocols for body recomposition and found: "Both a moderate energy deficit and a maintenance-calorie high-protein diet can elicit body recomposition... suggesting that elevated protein intake (2.5 g·kg⁻¹·d⁻¹) may facilitate simultaneous improvements in fat mass and FFM" ([source](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-026-06209-6)).

3. **The Bodybuilding Dietitians (2026)** summarize it well: "Body recomposition is most achievable for beginners, those returning to training after a break, individuals at higher body fat percentages, and those optimising key variables such as protein intake for the first time" ([source](https://www.thebodybuildingdietitians.com/blog/body-recomposition-guide-how-to-build-muscle-and-lose-fat-at-the-same-time)).

#### Why It Works for This Profile

Three factors make this individual an **ideal candidate** for body recomposition:

1. **Beginner status** — Untrained muscle tissue has high "anabolic sensitivity." The body responds to resistance training with pronounced adaptations that a trained individual cannot achieve.

2. **High body fat (40%+ BMI)** — Large fat stores provide a caloric reservoir. The body can mobilize stored fat to partially fuel muscle building, even in a slight caloric deficit. A lean person cannot do this efficiently.

3. **First-time protein optimization** — Someone who has never tracked protein intake and increases it to 1.6-2.4 g/kg will experience a significant boost in muscle protein synthesis from dietary change alone.

#### Evidence AGAINST (Nuances)

- Recomposition is **slower** than dedicated bulk/cut phases. Scale weight may barely move.
- As training experience increases (6-12+ months), recomposition becomes harder.
- Very lean individuals (<12% body fat men) cannot effectively recompose — they need surplus calories for muscle gain.

### How to Optimize for Both Simultaneously

#### The Recomposition Formula

| Variable | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Caloric Intake** | Maintenance or 100-300 kcal below maintenance | Mild deficit allows fat loss while stored fat fuels muscle building |
| **Protein** | 1.6-2.4 g/kg of body weight (see Section 4) | Drives muscle protein synthesis; protective in a deficit |
| **Resistance Training** | 3-4 sessions per week, progressive overload | The stimulus that triggers muscle growth |
| **Sleep** | 7-9 hours per night | Where actual recovery and growth occur |
| **Cardio** | Optional, low-intensity (walking) | Increases caloric expenditure without interfering with recovery |
| **Patience** | 3-6 month timeline | Recomposition is slow but sustainable |

#### Caloric Approach for This Individual

At 130 kg, maintenance calories are likely around **2,800-3,200 kcal/day** (sedentary to lightly active BMR + TEF + daily movement).

**Recommended starting point:**
- Week 1-4: **Eat at maintenance** — focus on protein targets and training consistency
- Week 5-12: **Slight deficit of 200-400 kcal** — prioritize protein, maintain training
- Month 4+: Adjust based on progress (if scale dropping and strength increasing, stay course)

> **Do NOT crash diet.** Aggressive caloric restriction (>500 kcal deficit) will impair muscle building, increase injury risk, and is unsustainable. Slow and steady wins.

## 4. Protein & Nutrition for Hypertrophy

### Optimal Protein Intake

#### What the Research Says

The Morton et al. (2018) meta-analysis — the most widely cited paper on this topic — analyzed 49 studies with 1,863 participants and found that protein supplementation enhanced resistance training-induced gains in fat-free mass, with a suggested breakpoint at **~1.62 g/kg/day** ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28698222/)).

However, **Stronger by Science's deep reanalysis (2024)** revealed critical issues with this breakpoint:

- The breakpoint (1.62 g/kg) was **not statistically significant** (p = 0.079)
- The 95% confidence interval spanned **1.03 to 2.20 g/kg** — almost the entire range studied
- Supplementary figures showed that subjects already consuming ≥1.6 g/kg **still benefited from additional protein**
- The analysis may suffer from Simpson's Paradox — cross-study trends differ from within-study effects

**Conclusion from SBS:** "Slightly higher intakes may be necessary to max out your gains" than the commonly cited 1.6 g/kg ([source](https://www.strongerbyscience.com/protein-science/)).

#### Practical Protein Targets for a 130 kg Beginner

| Goal | Target | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Minimum effective** | 1.6 g/kg = **208 g/day** | Based on Morton meta-analysis lower bound |
| **Optimal for recomposition** | 2.0-2.4 g/kg = **260-312 g/day** | Higher end protects muscle in a deficit; aids satiety |
| **Practical target** | **200-220 g/day** | Using ~1.6-1.7 g/kg; achievable and evidence-based |

> **Note on g/kg calculations:** For obese individuals, using total body weight overestimates protein needs because fat mass is metabolically inactive. Two approaches:
> - **Total body weight approach:** 1.6-2.0 g/kg × 130 kg = 208-260 g/day
> - **Lean body mass approach (more accurate):** Estimated LBM ~75-85 kg → 2.0-2.5 g/kg LBM = 150-213 g/day
>
> **Recommendation:** Target **180-220 g/day** — a practical, sustainable range that covers all bases.

### Protein Timing & Distribution

- **Muscle protein synthesis (MPS)** is maximized by **0.3-0.4 g/kg per meal** (≈40-55 g for this individual)
- **Distribute across 3-5 meals** spaced 3-4 hours apart
- **Pre-workout:** 30-40 g protein within 2 hours before training
- **Post-workout:** 30-40 g protein within 2 hours after training (the "anabolic window" is wider than previously thought — 4-6 hours)
- **Before bed:** 30-40 g slow-digesting protein (casein, Greek yogurt, paneer) to support overnight MPS

| Breakfast (8 AM) | 40 g | 3 eggs + 100 g Greek yogurt + 1 scoop whey |
| Lunch (12 PM) | 50 g | 200 g chicken breast + rice + dal |

## 6. Recovery

### Why Recovery Is Different at 130 kg

Carrying significant excess weight affects recovery in several ways:

1. **Higher systemic inflammation** — Adipose tissue produces pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) that can impair muscle protein synthesis and lengthen recovery time.
2. **Greater joint stress per session** — Even machine-based training creates more mechanical stress on load-bearing joints at higher body weight.
3. **Sleep disturbances** — Obesity is strongly associated with obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), which fragments sleep and impairs recovery. **If you snore heavily or wake up tired, get a sleep study done.**
4. **Lower work capacity** — Cardiovascular fitness is likely low; sessions will feel harder and require more recovery between them.
5. **Insulin resistance** — Common with Class III obesity; impairs nutrient partitioning (more energy stored as fat, less toward muscle).

### Sleep — The #1 Recovery Tool

Sleep is when **the majority of muscle repair and growth occurs**. Key processes during sleep:

- **Growth hormone** is released in pulses during deep (slow-wave) sleep — critical for tissue repair
- **Testosterone** production peaks during REM sleep
- **Muscle protein synthesis** is elevated during sleep, especially when pre-sleep protein is consumed
- **Cortisol** (catabolic hormone) is lowest during sleep; sleep deprivation raises cortisol

**Sleep deprivation effects on hypertrophy:**
- Reduces testosterone by 10-15% after one week of 5-hour nights (Leproult & Van Cauter, 2011)
- Increases cortisol and inflammatory markers
- Significantly reduces muscle protein synthesis
- Impairs cognitive function → worse technique → higher injury risk

**Recommendations:**
- **Target 8-9 hours per night** (more than the general 7-hour recommendation, because recovery demands are higher during initial training)
- Consistent sleep/wake times (regulate circadian rhythm)
- Cool, dark room
- No screens 60 minutes before bed
- If sleep apnea is suspected — **this is a medical priority**. CPAP treatment dramatically improves body composition, energy, and training results.

### Rest Days

| Phase | Training Days | Rest Days | Minimum Rest Between Sessions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weeks 1-4 | 3 per week | 4 per week | 48 hours |
| Weeks 5-12 | 3 per week | 4 per week | 48 hours |
| Month 4+ (4-day split) | 4 per week | 3 per week | 24 hours (upper/lower allows this) |

**Never train the same muscle group on consecutive days** during the beginner phase.

### Managing DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness)

DOMS is normal for beginners — it peaks 24-72 hours after a session and gradually diminishes over days.

**What helps:**
- **Active recovery:** Light walking (10-20 min) on rest days improves blood flow
- **Adequate protein:** Reduces muscle damage markers
- **Sleep:** Primary recovery mechanism
- **Stretching/Mobility:** Gentle, not aggressive
- **Warm baths/Epsom salt:** May reduce perceived soreness
- **Massage/Foam rolling:** Modest evidence but feels good; may reduce tightness

**What doesn't help much:**
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen) — may actually blunt hypertrophy by reducing inflammation needed for adaptation
- Ice baths — similarly may interfere with muscle growth signaling
- Complete inactivity — worsens stiffness

**When DOMS is concerning:**
- If soreness is so severe you can't straighten your arms/legs → may be rhabdomyolysis (seek medical attention)
- If soreness lasts more than 5 days → likely trained too hard; reduce volume next session
- Joint pain (not muscle soreness) → different problem; modify exercise

### Deloading

A **deload week** is a planned reduction in training volume/intensity to allow accumulated fatigue to dissipate.

**For beginners, deload every 6-8 weeks:**
- Reduce all working sets by 50% (e.g., 3 sets → 1-2 sets)
- Reduce load by 10-20%
- Keep the same exercises and rep ranges
- Purpose: Allow connective tissue, nervous system, and muscles to fully recover

**Signs you need a deload sooner:**
- Strength regression (getting weaker)
- Persistent fatigue / poor sleep
- Loss of motivation
- Niggling aches in joints
- Decreased appetite

## 7. Progress Tracking

### Why the Scale Lies During Recomposition

During body recomposition, the scale is the **least useful metric**. Here's why:

If you lose 1 kg of fat and gain 1 kg of muscle in a month:
- **Scale weight: 0 change** → looks like "no progress"
- **Reality: Significant body composition improvement**

This is the #1 reason people abandon recomposition — they think nothing is happening.

### What to Track Instead

#### Tier 1 — Most Important

| Metric | How Often | What to Look For |
|---|---|---|
| **Strength progression** | Every session | Weight going up or reps going up = muscle is growing |
| **Progress photos** | Every 2 weeks, same lighting/pose/time of day | Visual changes in body shape |
| **Tape measurements** | Weekly: waist, chest, hips, arms, thighs | Waist going down while chest/arms going up = recomposition |
| **How clothes fit** | Ongoing | Looser waist, tighter sleeves/chest = recomposition |

#### Tier 2 — Helpful

| Metric | How Often | What to Look For |
|---|---|---|
| **Scale weight (weekly average)** | Daily weigh, weekly average | Slow downward trend (0.2-0.5 kg/week is good) |
| **Body fat % (bioimpedance)** | Monthly | Inaccurate but trend direction is useful |
| **Workout performance** | Every session | Are sets getting easier? Can you do more reps? |

#### Tier 3 — Nice to Have

| Metric | Notes |
|---|---|
| **DEXA scan** | Gold standard but expensive (~₹2000-5000 in India); do once at start, once at 6 months |
| **Bloodwork** | Track testosterone, lipid panel, HbA1c, vitamin D at start and 3-6 months in |

### Measurement Protocol

**Take baseline measurements before starting:**

1. **Weigh yourself** every morning after using the bathroom, before eating/drinking. Record daily, average weekly.
2. **Measure circumference** with a soft tape measure:
   - Waist: At navel
   - Chest: At nipple line
   - Hips: Widest point
   - Right arm: Flexed, widest point
   - Right thigh: Widest point
3. **Photos:** Front, side, and back. Same time of day, same lighting, same pose. Flexed and relaxed.
4. **Record starting weights** for every exercise in your program.

### What Success Looks Like in 12 Weeks

| Indicator | Good Progress | Excellent Progress |
|---|---|---|
| Scale weight | -2 to -6 kg | -4 to -8 kg |
| Waist circumference | -3 to -7 cm | -5 to -10 cm |
| Chest measurement | +1 to +3 cm | +2 to +4 cm |
| Arm measurement | +0.5 to +1.5 cm | +1 to +2 cm |
| Strength on key lifts | +20-40% | +30-60% |
| Progress photos | Visible reduction in midsection; more defined arms/chest | Dramatic visual change |
| How clothes fit | Looser at waist | Noticeably different fit everywhere |

> **Note:** At 130 kg, the scale *can* and *should* trend downward (unlike a lean person recompositioning). But strength should simultaneously go UP. If scale drops but strength also drops significantly, protein or calories may be too low.

## 8. Recommended 12-Week Starter Program

### Program Overview

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Split** | Full Body, 3 days per week |
| **Sessions per week** | 3 (with at least 1 rest day between) |
| **Session duration** | 45-60 minutes |
| **Rep range** | 10-15 (majority in 10-12) |
| **RIR target** | Weeks 1-4: RIR 2-3; Weeks 5-8: RIR 1-2; Weeks 9-12: RIR 1 |
| **Progression model** | Double progression (see below) |
| **Deload** | Week 7 (planned) |

### Progression Model: Double Progression

This is the simplest and most effective progression scheme for beginners:

1. Pick a **target rep range** for each exercise (e.g., 10-12 reps)
2. Start with a weight you can do for the **bottom of the range** (10 reps) at the target RIR
3. Each session, try to **add 1 rep** to each set
4. When you hit the **top of the range** (12 reps) on ALL sets with the target RIR, **increase the weight** by the smallest increment next session
5. Repeat

**Example for Leg Press (target 10-12 reps):**
- Week 1: 100 kg × 10, 10, 10 (RIR 2)
- Week 2: 100 kg × 11, 10, 10
- Week 3: 100 kg × 12, 11, 10
- Week 4: 100 kg × 12, 12, 12 → **Increase weight**
- Week 5: 110 kg × 10, 10, 9 → Start over

### Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

**Goal:** Learn movements, build tissue tolerance, establish the habit.

#### Workout B (e.g., Wednesday)
| # | Exercise | Sets | Reps | Rest | RIR |

| 1 | Box Squat (bodyweight or light DB) | 2 | 10 | 120s | 3 |
| 2 | Incline Machine Press | 2 | 12 | 90s | 3 |
| 3 | Lat Pulldown (close grip) | 2 | 12 | 90s | 3 |

**Total weekly volume:** ~8 sets per major muscle group.  
**Cardio:** 15-20 min walk after training (optional, beneficial).  
**On rest days:** 20-30 min gentle walk.

### Phase 2: Build (Weeks 5-8)

**Goal:** Increase volume slightly, push closer to failure, begin progressive overload in earnest.

*Same exercises as Phase 1, but:*
- **Increase all exercises to 3 sets** (from 2)
- **RIR drops to 1-2** (push harder)
- **Rest stays the same**
- **Week 7 is a deload week:** All sets reduced to 1-2, load reduced by 20%, RIR 3+

**Total weekly volume:** ~12 sets per major muscle group.

### Phase 3: Accelerate (Weeks 9-12)

**Goal:** Maximize progressive overload, prepare for transition to 4-day split.

*Same exercises, but:*
- Keep 3 sets per exercise
- **RIR drops to 1** (very close to failure, especially on last set)
- **Add intensity on last sets:** Last set of each exercise can go to RIR 0 (failure) on isolation exercises only (curls, pushdowns, leg extension, face pulls)
- Consider adding a 4th set to 1-2 key compound lifts (leg press, chest press, pulldown)

**Total weekly volume:** ~12-15 sets per major muscle group.

### Week-by-Week Progression Summary

| Week | Phase | Sets/Exercise | RIR | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Foundation | 2 | 3 | Very easy; focus on form |
| 2 | Foundation | 2 | 3 | Start finding right weights |
| 3 | Foundation | 2 | 2-3 | Weights should feel challenging but controllable |
| 4 | Foundation | 2 | 2 | Solidify technique |
| 5 | Build | 3 | 2 | Volume increase; expect more soreness |
| 6 | Build | 3 | 1-2 | First real "hard" training week |
| **7** | **DELOAD** | **1-2** | **3+** | **Reduce load 20%; recover** |
| 8 | Build | 3 | 1-2 | Return to pre-deload weights |
| 9 | Accelerate | 3 | 1 | Push hard; progressive overload |
| 10 | Accelerate | 3 | 1 | Push hard; beat last week's numbers |
| 11 | Accelerate | 3-4 | 1 | Add a set to 1-2 compound lifts |
| 12 | Accelerate | 3-4 | 1 | Final week; test new maxes; plan next phase |

### What to Do After Week 12

1. **Take baseline measurements again** (weigh, measure, photos)
2. **Compare to starting point** — celebrate progress
3. **Transition to 4-day Upper/Lower split** (Section 5) for months 4-6
4. **Adjust calories** based on progress:
   - If scale dropped >6 kg and strength improved → maintain same calories
   - If scale barely moved but measurements improved → slight deficit (200-300 kcal)
   - If scale dropped but strength decreased → increase calories by 200-300
5. **Consider adding cardio** (20-30 min Zone 2, 2-3×/week) for cardiovascular health and additional fat loss

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start with machines.** They're safe, scalable, and effective for a 130 kg beginner. Free weights come later.
2. **Progressive overload is everything.** More reps or more weight every week = muscle growth.
3. **Body recomposition will happen** — you're the ideal candidate (beginner + high body fat + optimizing protein). Don't stress the scale.
4. **Eat 180-220 g protein/day.** This is non-negotiable for muscle growth. Spread across 4-5 meals.
5. **Sleep 8-9 hours.** This is where growth happens. If you suspect sleep apnea, get checked.
6. **Train 3 days per week** for the first 12 weeks. Consistency beats intensity.
7. **Track everything.** Photos, measurements, strength numbers. The scale alone will mislead you.
8. **Be patient.** Recomposition is slow. 12 weeks is the beginning, not the end. Real transformation happens over 12-24 months.
